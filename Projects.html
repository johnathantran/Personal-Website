REVERSE ENGINEERED PEPPER GRINDER

In my Introduction to Engineering Design and Graphics class in freshman year, I joined a team of three other students to reverse engineer a small mechanical device. My team selected a mechanical pepper grinder. Our task was to create 3D models of each individual part using Solidworks, 3D print these models, and assemble a rapid prototype of the final device.


Housing and Core

I designed the housing and core of the pepper grinder using SolidWorks. This is the mechanism that actually performs the grinding of the pepper granules.

SPRING LOADED BALL LAUNCHER

In my Dynamics class sophomore year, I teamed up with two other students to produce a spring loaded tennis ball launcher. The goal had two parts:

1. Use the launcher to propel a tennis ball to hit a target 1.5 m away and 20 cm tall

2. Avoid a 20 cm tall barrier that would be placed at the half distance mark.

To achieve this, we had to calculate spring displacement and angle, accounting for variables such as energy loss, mass of the ball and its cradle, and stiffness of the spring. To model the process, we applied principles of kinematics and kinetics of particle movement. The calculation basis was simple: apply kinematics to find the necessary velocity of the projectile, then use conservation of energy to find the necessary spring compression.


As seen, the launcher operates using a pawl locked into a threaded rod to hold the spring in tension. When the pawl is released, the launcher fires the ball. Our team was given a budget of $50 for this project, but we chose to 3D print the entire device for cost efficiency.
