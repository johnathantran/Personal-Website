MECHANICAL ENGINEERING LABS

Here, I present a brief overview of my undergraduate lab work at UT Austin. I actually gave a lot of thought before including this section, because who really wants to read about labs? But, I eventually became sure it would be a good idea to document my undergraduate experience. Not just for future employers to better visualize the breadth of my coursework, but for myself as well. To remind myself of what I've learned, and how as a future engineer, I can harness my knowledge of how the natural world works in creative, inspired ways. And as much work as labs are (and I really mean hours and hours... and hours... of work), they allowed me to learn a lot in terms of how I can apply what I learned in class practically. So here they are.


MATERIALS ENGINEERING LAB

In Materials lab (designated ME 134L at UT Austin), we studied the properties of materials commonly used in manufacturing and design, such as polymers, brasses, and steels. By performing tests on the mechanical properties of these materials, observing their microstructures and processing techniques, we were able to understand how to select the best materials for any desired quality in a product. Although we tested many different materials throughout the course of this lab, all the data and images provided below are only from our work with 4140 steel samples, which I believe accurately represents the cumulation of everything that I've learned in this course.

In our testing of 4140 steel, we learned how the mechanical properties of different steels depend on what heat treatment and processing techniques are used. The different microstructures we obtained and tested in this lab are spheroidite, coarse and fine pearlite, martensite, and tempered martensite. A guide to the heat treatment processes we performed in this lab is shown below, along with a relevant TTT diagram:

Our samples were created by first heat treating and processing an austenized steel bar to produce the desired microstructure outlined above. To prepare our sample for microstructural study, we performed the following steps to the steel specimen. We sectioned the bar with a cut-off wheel, mounted the sample with a thermosetting resin, polished and grinded it thoroughly, and finally applied a chemical etchant to enhance contrast under the SEM. We also performed hardness, tensile, and impact tests on our samples along with studying them under the SEM to match certain microstructures to certain mechanical properties. Shown below are the instruments and data from these tests:

From left to right, the Wilson Rockwell Hardness Tester, Instron 5800 Materials Testing system, and Tinius Olsen Impact Testing machine.

The results from these tests prompted a deeper investigation into why these materials exhibited certain mechanical properties. We then studied a variety of samples under a SEM and optical microscope to obtain micrographs that would explain why a certain material was stronger or more ductile than another. Shown below are the SEM micrographs we obtained from our final samples of each steel microstructure:

EXPERIMENTAL FLUID MECHANICS

In fluids lab, we put into practice the principles learned from our fluid mechanics lecture course. We used experimental instrumentation such as a viscometer, water channel, and wind tunnel. We studied and practiced flow measurement techniques and flow visualizaton. Our labs focused particularly on how flow interacts with solid bodies and analyzing data to draw conclusions about the flow. Examples of the different flow visualization techniques we used are shown below:

Flow characteristics observed with water table and dye.
Flow characteristics observed with wind tunnel and smoke generator.

We also extensively studied wind tunnel measurements of wake and drag for a cylinder in cross-flow. We collected data using a wind tunnel for the velocity and pressure profiles around the cylinder, and calculated drag force using this data through Bernoulli's equation and numerical integration. Sample data of the profiles we graphed are shown below:

Plot of velocity for each height location for both upstream and downstream taken at a wind tunnel velocity of 30 m/s.
Plot of pressure around the cylinder in kPa for each angle increment of 10 degrees from 0 to 180 degrees for both 15 m/s and 30 m/s wind tunnel air speeds.

Finally, we also studied lift and drag forces on three vehicle models: the McLaren Mercedes, the Chevy Camaro, and the Ford F150. By applying dimensional analysis, we were able to predict flow behavior of the actual vehicle prototypes. Sample data from these experiments are shown below:

Plot of Reynolds number at different wind speeds in the 24” wind tunnel vs the lift coefficient for the three test models: the Ford F150 with the tail up, the Ford F150 with the tail down, and the Mercedes.
Plot of Reynolds number at different wind speeds in the 24” wind tunnel vs the drag coefficient for the three test models: the Ford F150 with the tail up, the Ford F150 with the tail down, and the Mercedes.
